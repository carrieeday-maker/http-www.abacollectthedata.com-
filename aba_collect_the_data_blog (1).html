Method 01 · Frequency & Event Recording
How to Count Behaviors: A Beginner's Guide to Frequency Recording
The simplest and most common form of ABA data collection. If you can count on your fingers, you already understand the concept. Here's everything you need to get started.
RBTs BCBAs Parents Beginner-friendly ▶ 2 Videos
▼
What is Frequency Recording?

Frequency recording (also called event recording) is simply counting how many times a behavior happens during an observation period. Every time the target behavior occurs, you mark it down — a tally mark, a tap on a counter app, or a click on a wrist counter.

At the end of the session, you have a number: the total count. You can also calculate a rate by dividing the count by the number of minutes observed, which makes it easier to compare sessions of different lengths.

When should you use it?
✅
Best used when the behavior has a clear beginning and end — like hitting, calling out, requesting an item, saying a word, or throwing an object. If you can clearly tell when the behavior started and stopped, frequency recording is a great fit.
⚠️
Not ideal for behaviors that last a long time — like a 20-minute tantrum or extended stereotypy. For those, Duration Recording is a better choice.
Who uses this?
🧑‍⚕️ RBTs tracking problem behaviors 👩‍💼 BCBAs monitoring treatment progress 👨‍👩‍👧 Parents at home counting requests or outbursts 🏫 Teachers in the classroom
Step-by-step: How to collect frequency data
1
Define the behavior clearly — write a precise, observable definition. Instead of "aggression," write "any instance of hitting another person with an open or closed hand."
2
Set your observation period — decide when you'll start and stop collecting (e.g., during the 30-minute session, or during dinner time at home).
3
Every time the behavior occurs, record it — tap the counter, make a tally mark, or press the button in the digital tool below.
4
At the end, count the total and optionally divide by session minutes to get a rate per minute.
5
Record your session info (client name, date, therapist) so data can be tracked over time and shared with your team or BCBA.
Tips for accuracy
💡
Only count complete instances. If the behavior starts but you're unsure if it meets your definition, don't count it — consistency is more important than capturing every borderline case.
📌
Use the undo button. Accidental taps happen. The digital tool below has an undo function so you don't have to restart your session over a miscount.
Common behaviors tracked with frequency recording

Hitting, kicking, biting, vocal outbursts, elopement attempts, self-injurious behavior (SIB), unprompted requests (mands), eye contact initiations, appropriate greetings, and on-task behavior.

Method 02 · Duration Recording
How Long Did It Last? A Guide to Duration Recording
When the number of times something happens matters less than how long it goes on, duration recording is your tool. Learn how to time behaviors accurately and what the data tells you.
RBTs BCBAs Parents Time-based ▶ 2 Videos
▼
What is Duration Recording?

Duration recording measures how long a behavior lasts from the moment it starts to the moment it ends. You start a stopwatch when the behavior begins and stop it when it ends. Each occurrence is called an episode.

At the end of a session you can calculate: total duration (all episodes added together), mean duration (average episode length), and percentage of session time the behavior occupied.

When should you use it?
✅
Best for behaviors that vary in length — tantrums, crying episodes, time spent on-task, engagement with an activity, stereotypy that runs continuously, or elopement. The question you're trying to answer is "how long," not just "how often."
⚠️
Harder to use when behaviors are very brief or frequent — like rapid vocal tics or quick taps. For those, frequency recording is easier to manage in real time.
Who uses this?
🧑‍⚕️ RBTs timing tantrum episodes 👩‍💼 BCBAs analyzing engagement time 👨‍👩‍👧 Parents tracking meltdown length at home
Step-by-step: How to collect duration data
1
Define the behavior's start and stop rules — e.g., "tantrum begins when the child falls to the floor or begins crying loudly; ends when child is calm and upright for 10 consecutive seconds."
2
When the behavior begins, start your timer. In the digital tool, press the large "▶ Start" button on the behavior card.
3
When the behavior ends, press Stop. The episode is saved automatically with its start time and duration.
4
Repeat for each episode. Multiple episodes can be tracked for the same behavior across a session.
5
Review the summary tab at the end to see total duration, mean duration, and percentage of session time per behavior.
Understanding your data
📈
Mean duration going down is a good sign. If tantrums were averaging 12 minutes and are now averaging 5 minutes, that's meaningful progress — even if the number of tantrums stayed the same.
📊
Percentage of session time is one of the most useful metrics for behaviors like on-task engagement. A goal of "80% of session time on-task" is specific, measurable, and easy to track with this method.

Method 03 · Interval Recording
Partial vs. Whole Interval: Understanding Interval Recording
Interval recording lets you watch multiple behaviors at once without needing to count every single occurrence. It's the go-to method when you need efficiency without sacrificing meaningful data.
RBTs BCBAs Intermediate Multi-behavior ▶ 2 Videos
▼
What is Interval Recording?

Instead of counting every occurrence or timing every episode, interval recording divides your observation period into equal time blocks (intervals) — typically 10, 15, or 30 seconds long. At the end of each interval, you simply note: did the behavior occur or not?

This gives you an estimated percentage of intervals during which the behavior was observed — a very practical measure that's much easier to collect when you're also delivering instruction.

Partial vs. Whole Interval — what's the difference?
📌
Partial Interval: Mark "Yes" if the behavior occurred at any point during the interval — even just for a second. This tends to overestimate how much the behavior is happening. Best for behaviors you want to increase (like appropriate requests) so you don't miss any occurrences.
📌
Whole Interval: Mark "Yes" only if the behavior lasted the entire interval without stopping. This tends to underestimate behavior. Best for behaviors you want to increase in duration (like sustained on-task behavior or play engagement).
When should you use it?
📍 Behaviors that are hard to count discretely 📍 When you need to track multiple behaviors at once 📍 When you're also delivering instruction during the session 📍 Classroom and group settings
Step-by-step: How to collect interval data
1
Set your interval length — 10 seconds is common for fast-moving sessions; 30 seconds works well for calmer environments. The tool lets you choose anything from 5–300 seconds.
2
Set your session length — this determines how many total intervals you'll have. A 10-minute session with 10-second intervals = 60 intervals.
3
Choose Partial or Whole interval — or select "Both" to track the same behavior with both methods side-by-side for comparison.
4
Press Start and observe. The countdown timer tells you when each interval ends. Mark Yes or No for each behavior before the next interval begins.
5
At the end, review % occurrence — the number of "Yes" intervals divided by total intervals gives you your percentage score for that session.
Important note for parents & caregivers
💡
Interval recording is best done with some practice first. If you're new to it, try a 30-second interval length while tracking just one behavior before adding more. The digital tool's audio cues (the countdown turning red) help you stay on pace.

Method 04 · Task Analysis & Chaining
Teaching Step by Step: Task Analysis, Chaining & Prompt Tracking
When the goal is teaching a multi-step skill — like hand washing, getting dressed, or making a snack — task analysis breaks it into manageable pieces and tracks exactly how much help is needed at each step.
RBTs BCBAs Parents Skill building ▶ 1 Video
▼
What is Task Analysis?

Task analysis means breaking a complex skill down into its individual steps. Instead of recording "washed hands: yes or no," you record each component separately — turning on the water, applying soap, scrubbing, rinsing, drying — and note how much help was needed for each one.

This gives your team a precise picture of which parts of a skill have been mastered and which still need work.

The three chaining methods — which one to use?
➡️
Forward Chaining: Teach the first step to independence before moving on. Best when step 1 is the most natural starting point and the learner will benefit from early wins at the beginning of the chain.
⬅️
Backward Chaining: Teach the last step first. The learner always gets to experience the sense of completion. Great for routines where the ending moment is motivating (e.g., putting on a shirt and "done!").
🔄
Total Task Chaining: The learner attempts every step every session, with prompts provided wherever needed. Best for learners who can handle the whole routine but need fading support across all steps simultaneously.
Understanding prompt levels

For each step, record the least intrusive prompt used to get the learner to complete that step:

I
Independent (I) — completed without any help. This is the goal!
VP
Verbal Prompt (VP) — a spoken instruction or cue was needed, like "Now rinse your hands."
GP
Gestural Prompt (GP) — a point, nod, or gesture directed the learner to the next step.
PP
Partial Physical (PP) — a light touch or partial hand-over-hand was needed.
FP
Full Physical (FP) — full hand-over-hand assistance was required to complete the step.
X
Did Not Complete (X) — the learner did not complete the step even with maximum prompting.
Step-by-step: How to collect task analysis data
1
Build your task analysis — list every step in order. Use one of the pre-built templates (hand washing, tooth brushing, etc.) or create your own. Be specific: each step should be a single observable action.
2
Choose your chaining method — Forward, Backward, or Total Task. If you're unsure, Total Task is a safe default for most learners.
3
Run through the routine with the learner. For each step, use the least intrusive prompt that results in the behavior, then tap the corresponding prompt level button (I, VP, GP, PP, FP, or X).
4
Add a new session for each session date. Over time, you'll see prompt levels shift from FP → PP → GP → VP → I as the learner gains independence.
5
Review the Summary tab to see each step's % independence across all sessions — a clear picture of what's mastered and what still needs work.
Tips for parents & caregivers
🏠
Consistency is everything. Use the same steps in the same order every time. Even small changes (using a different sink, a different soap bottle) can affect performance. Share your task analysis with school staff and other caregivers so everyone is using the same sequence.
🌟
Celebrate independent steps. Even if your child needed a full physical prompt for 9 out of 10 steps, an independent step 3 is a genuine win worth celebrating enthusiastically.
